Beyond-the-Screen Design: Site-Specific Installation

Exploring the integration of virtual reality, object-tracking, and sensor-based interaction in site-specific installations.

By Ewan Blake · MAY 2, 2024

Introduction

This report will conceptualise a beyond-the-screen design using site-specific installation. The report will briefly discuss defining what site-specific installation is, and how it can enhance the user experience of an already-developed application. Then, grounded in academic literature, it will delve into the merits and limitations of site-specific installation, providing examples of where this technology has been implemented. After that, discussions of user engagement strategies and considerations for the design will be had, before finally coming to a sound conclusion and a proposed design for how this can be integrated into the context of the beyond-the-screen design.

According to Salgueiro (2023), site-specific installations are designs typically created to have an intimate connection with the environment, typically physical and one the audience can interact with. Generally, site-specific installations have been connected with thematically historical content, as being able to recreate something accurately and physically can provide an effective emotional experience for newer generations. However, it is hoped that by combining the technologies site-specific installation can offer and the proposed design beyond the screen, a compelling narrative that fits the target audience can be created through the technological capabilities of virtual reality, sensor capabilities, and physical environments to provide an engaging and effective querying service. The technologies that will be discussed to bring this together include the following; Virtual reality, object-tracking and sensor-based interaction.

Virtual Reality (VR)

VR is seen as a piece of technology that has transformative versatility, by creating a new medium by which many elements such as storytelling, entertainment and exploration can be exploited by the use of this technology (LaValle, 2023). LaValle (2023) emphasizes the experiences on offer by exploiting these elements by saying the following:

VR is like a waking dream that could take place in a magical cartoon-like world or could transport us to another part of the Earth or universe.
LaValle, 2023

Because of the versatility of use in many sectors, the discussion of theoretically implementing this piece of technology into the education sector is a valid one. LaValle (2023) and a systematic review carried out by Lampropoulos and Kinshuk (2024) helped to strengthen further the potential of implementing VR into the education sector, highlighting benefits such as enhanced learning experiences, personalisation of said experiences, and the increase of engagement and motivation through the use of collaboration that can help to enhance the overall experience of the user.

However, despite the promising potential of the versatile collaboration and learning experience VR can bring, there are still limitations to what it can do. A journal article written by Fitria (2023) helped to outline the general limitations that hold back VR from being effectively integrated into the education sector. Current challenges highlighted include financial barriers; in the sense of developing and building these technologies, which in turn affects the final price of the product, which can then lead to accessibility issues amongst schools who may be able to afford it, despite potentially being able to benefit off of the use of it significantly.

To further identify the current journey of VR, comparing the limitations it experiences with the Gartner Hyper Cycle can be made. According to Gartner (2018), the Gartner Hype Cycle has 5 stages to document the life cycle of a technology, or in this case, a transitional technology like VR. Referring back to Fitria (2023), the availability of funds and lack of investment can be linked to the “innovation trigger” phase, where a generalised lack of investment and funds hinders the technology from being able to reach its full potential, affecting and all future development of this technology. The quality of the created technology and integration into the education sector may not match the predicted expectations of customers, harkening back to lack of investment affecting its future development, which relates to the “peak of inflated experience”.

Regarding site-specific installations, VR offers opportunistic transformative flexibility for immersive experiences that exploit multiple engaging elements; storytelling, entertainment and exploration. Despite this, certain limitations such as the cost of the product and the cost of making the product can hamper the accessibility of the product, and therefore limit the potential impact VR could have on varying sectors.

Object-Tracking (OT)

The main objective of visual object tracking is that the marked object with coordinate, width and height in the initial frame, is desired to be tracked in the following frames, that is to say, the trajectory of the object should be predicted (Zhang, J., Sun, J., Wang, J., Li, Z, & Chen, X., 2022).

The above explains, much like VR, that it is a very flexible technology that can be used across many different sectors. Examples include surveillance and security; tracking and monitoring specific individuals in public spaces or tracking intruders through CCTV.

In the context of OT combined with VR, it is important to acknowledge the ability to simulate a first-person-view (FPV), with these combined technologies. VR aims to simulate a real-life environment in a virtualised state. OT can take that a step further by adding an integral sense of touch. By aligning objects within the world of VR with real-life objects, it further embraces a person into the world they are being simulated in. It is important to look at the benefits and limitations OT can currently bring when being used. A paper by Matteo Dunnhofer, Giovanni Maria Farinella & Micheloni (2022) analysed the capabilities of OT using the TREK-150 benchmark. TREK-150 is a thoroughly described dataset aiming to benchmark the ongoing progress of visual OT (Matteo Dunnhofer et al., 2022). Benefits of OT based on this paper include improvement in the referral of existing objects, localization and efficiency over existing object detection methods.

Regarding limitations, a paper from Mirzaei, Nezamabadi-pour, Raoof & Derakhshani (2023) helped to acknowledge some potential limitations of various methodologies of OT. Due to the context of combining it with VR, this study will focus primarily on two methods brought up in this specific paper; track-by-detection and filter-based. Track-by-detection can help to automatically initialise and manage scenes with a large number of objects simultaneously through the use of detection algorithms (Mirzaei et al., 2023). This is generally good as it can help simplify the development process, improve the user's general experience, and allow for more dynamic and responsive virtual environments, all thanks to the technology being able to run more smoothly and handle more objects. However, track-by-detection relies heavily on the quality and performance of object detection, meaning that if the performance and quality are low, this can lead to poor tracking performance (Mirzaei et al., 2023). If this were to happen, it would significantly reduce the usability and immersive experience that the VR technology would output to the user, further emphasizing the importance of an effective object detector algorithm.

The filter-based Kalman filter can enhance tracking efficiency through optimally-based estimations that do not require a ton of computational requirements, meaning that this particular method can be considered suitable for real-time responsiveness (Mirzaei et al., 2023). However, the filter method assumes the use of Gaussian distribution, also now more commonly known as normal distribution, and the method itself is generally only suitable for linear systems (Mirzaei et al., 2023). This could lead to inaccuracies being present in the FPV or limitations being present, specifically in contexts where the movement of objects, users, or both are non-linear.

The integration of OT with VR in a site-specific installation context has the potential to create a wholly immersive experience. By leveraging interactivity and personalisation of an experience, designers can take advantage of the strengths of combining these technologies to create a potentially compelling installation to engage and inspire users in creative new ways.

Sensor-Based Interaction (SBI)

Interactive sensors are another piece of technology related to site-specific installation that is versatile across many different sectors, ranging back to the early 2000s. A few examples of this technology being implemented in diverse sectors include; military intelligence gathering (Livingston & Herbst, 2005) and human-machine interaction (Chen, J. -F., Li, M, -H., & Yeh, Hsin-I, 2013). The latter of these examples is relevant to the topic of SBI as human-machine interaction involves the process of integrating sensor technology within a real, physical space to create an immersive, interactive and reactive environment for users.

In a gamified context, the three most important interactive sensors are possibly motion, touch and gesture recognition sensors. A study carried out by Yang, S., Gao, Z., Hadi Mogavi, R., Hui, P., & Braud, T. (2023) designed and developed an immersive experience combining the digital capabilities of VR with the Web. In an educational context, the Tangible Web project deals with physical sensors, with which motion, touch and gesture respectively align with. Additionally, the theorization of the Tangible Web is done within the confinements of a biologically scientific example, however, this can still apply to the rest of the educational sector.

A study by Zhu, M., Sun, Z., & Lee, C (2021) relating to emphasises the need for accurate upper-limb detection in applications like VR and robotics, the former specifically. The paper itself provides some potential advantages of interactive sensors from the use of the exoskeleton suit. The proposed sensor they integrated into the suit allowed for multiple degrees of freedom (DOF) motions. This then allowed for accurate movement tracking in a variety of directions, therefore enhancing the functionality, effectiveness and immersion of the suit. Furthermore, a research paper carried out by Zinaty, M., El Ghaoui, E., Zeid Daou, R. A., Hayek, A., & Boercsoek, J (2023) explored combatting the limitations of mobile games to achieve a more immersive and interactive experience exploiting new transitional technologies. The research paper concludes that the addition of force and linear sensors, of which a flex sensor is attached to the game controller, allows for a more immersive and interactive gaming experience. The paper then goes on to mention the feasibility of expanding this experiment into the grounds of VR.

The integration of interactive sensors in site-specific installations can create immersive and interactive experiences, additionally across many sectors, but mainly education. The studies covered and discussed in this section highlighted the importance of specific sensors, motion, touch, and gesture, in enhancing human-machine interaction, and potentially applications that make use of VR. The adoption of interactive sensors into a site-specific installation can open up a wide range of possibilities for being able to create dynamic, responsive, and engaging experiences that mould together the physical and virtual realm to create a truly immersive experience.

Conclusion

Proposed Design

The proposed design for the site-specific installation is encapsulated inside of a large circular room. There will be around 6 different stations set out at equal space and footing across the room, in a circular shape outline. Users will be immersed with Meta Quest 2 headsets, due to affordability and therefore accessibility and will additionally be equipped with exoskeleton suits that will capture motion and replicate it within the VR world. All users will be connected to create a collaborative and social experience. Each stage will be met with an up-right rectangular wooden platform; which will exist on the physical and virtual realm, having been scanned in. The platforms themselves will be flat, however, they will contain maze-like routes that also exist within the physical and virtual realm.

Each stage will be linear, with the requirement of the previous task to be completed before a user can access and attempt the next one. Each time the user completes a stage, they will be met with a reactive cutscene. It is considered reactive in the context that it is directly linked to the user's headset, so when any avatars talk or move, they will look at and move around the user respectively.

Conclusion Of Work

The research conducted on VR, OT, and SBI in the merged context of a site-specific installation has helped to provide key understandings of where the benefits and limitations of these technologies may lie, and by extension how they might benefit, or negatively impact a site-specific installation. All of the research undertaken was done with a focus on the education sector, attempting to understand the current state of technology within that sector, how accessible it is, and how it might be used to benefit it in the long run.

VR states itself as an immersive and transformative tool, versatile in many sectors, specifically education. Studies by LaValle (2023), Lampropoulos, and Kinshuck (2024) helped to highlight its potential within the sector by possibly enhancing the experiences of learning, adding an element of personalisation to education, and increasing engagement in learning. However, it was noted by Fitria (2023) that financial and technological integration barriers remained significant challenges.

Integration of OT and SBI technologies respectively enables for the enhancement of the immersive experience provided by an educational site-specific installation. OT methods such as track-by-detection, allow for accurate object scanning into a virtual environment. SBI allows for motion, touch and gesture sensor recognition to be integrated, which was demonstrated in studies by Yang et al. (2023) and Zhu et al. (2021). By understanding how these technologies can integrate into a site-specific installation context, it was decided that by implementing Meta Quest 2 VR headsets with exoskeleton suits to allow for immersion and motion capture respectively, users would be able to engage dynamically and interactively, allowing for social collaboration with fellow users.

In conclusion, the research undertaken details the potential of exploring and using in tandem VR, OT, and SBI to help revolutionize education through immersive and interactive storytelling experiences. By addressing the issues and strengths of each technology identified from the academic research undertaken, a proposed design was created, with the hopes of creating a compelling, and immersive merged environment.
